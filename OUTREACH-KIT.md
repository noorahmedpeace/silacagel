These are pre-written statements the DryGelWorld Export Desk can hand to journalists writing trade-press features. Each quote is on a specific topic the desk can credibly speak to. Use **verbatim or lightly edited** — journalists prefer pre-cleared quotes because it saves them fact-checking time.

A single moldy carton of leather goods at destination — return logistics, replacement shipping, and the brand-relationship damage with the importing buyer — costs multiples of the savings on the desiccant line item. The math works in only one direction."
>
> — DryGelWorld Export Desk

### Quote: Cobalt chloride migration in indicating silica gel

> "Cobalt chloride blue indicating gel is migrating out of EU and UK markets and increasingly out of Australia and Canada under REACH-equivalent regulations. Orange indicating gel is the modern industrial standard. Buyers planning new programs should not specify blue regardless of whether their immediate market still permits it — global tightening is the direction of travel."
>
> — DryGelWorld Export Desk

### Quote: The pharma desiccant procurement gap

> "There's a recurring pattern in pharma packaging desiccant procurement: buyers conflate ISO 9001:2015 manufacturer certification with FDA Drug Master File or EU 1935/2004 food-contact compliance. They are completely different regulatory categories. Buyers who specify the wrong scope upfront discover the gap during quarterly audit, which is the worst possible time to fix it."
>
> — DryGelWorld Export Desk

### Quote: Karachi-to-Hamburg long-haul humidity

> "The route from Karachi to Northern European ports is one of the most aggressive condensation environments in commercial shipping — tropical origin humidity, multiple temperature transitions across the voyage, and 25-35 day duration. Cargo that survives this route reliably is cargo that received both per-carton sachet placement and container-ceiling desiccant strips, plus upstream packaging discipline. Sachets alone are not enough on this corridor."
>
> — DryGelWorld Export Desk

### Quote: Industrial PPE in food production

> "Bouffant hair nets and beard covers in food production aren't optional accessories — they're zone-coded hygiene compliance. Color matters: white, blue, green, and red zone systems are the standard pattern. Production buyers who don't enforce color-coded PPE typically also fail BRC and HACCP audits on related controls. The hygiene PPE program is a tell."
>
> — DryGelWorld Export Desk

### Quote: Bulk silica gel pricing transparency

> "Per-kg material cost varies less than fifteen percent across reputable global manufacturers at the same bead grade. When quoted prices vary by two-hundred to three-hundred percent for 'silica gel', the difference isn't the gel — it's the format, certification scope, MOQ commitment, and Incoterm. Buyers benchmarking quotes need to standardize all those factors before comparing per-kg numbers, otherwise they're comparing fundamentally different products."
>
> — DryGelWorld Export Desk

## 4. Guest post outlines (full structure, ready to draft from)

When a publication accepts the pitch, use the matching outline below as the draft scaffold.

### Outline A: "Container desiccant procurement checklist" (for Packaging Digest / Inbound Logistics)

1. **Lead** (150 words) — set up the buyer-side gap: industry talks specs, buyers need decisions.
2. **The 5-axis selection framework** — bead format, material, indicator, bead grade, regulatory scope.
3. **Sachet sizing math** — base calculation, sizing table by carton volume.
4. **Container loading reference** — strips per 20ft/40ft by route duration.
5. **Route multipliers** — what changes on trans-Pacific vs trans-equatorial vs intra-region.
6. **Packaging layer structure** — 5-layer reference (product, unit carton, master carton, pallet, container).
7. **Documentation requirements** — what to specify in the RFQ to avoid mid-program surprises.
8. **The 10-point pre-quote checklist** — actionable summary.
9. **Conclusion** — buyers who arrive prepared get usable quotes in 24-48h; buyers who don't spend 1-2 weeks in back-and-forth.

### Outline B: "Pharma desiccant: the procurement gap" (for Pharmaceutical Online)

1. **Lead** (150 words) — the ISO vs FDA DMF confusion.
2. **Direct-contact vs incidental-contact** — the most important distinction in pharma desiccant procurement.
3. **Regional regulatory frameworks** — FDA, EU 1935/2004, MHLW, GCC GSO.
4. **What FDA food-contact actually requires** — FCN/GRAS documentation.
5. **What EU 1935/2004 actually requires** — DoC under Article 16, migration testing.
6. **Contamination control** — the audit-finding question buyers forget to ask.
7. **Sachet format selection** — paper vs non-woven vs Tyvek.
8. **The buyer's checklist** — scoping certifications before scoping suppliers.
9. **Conclusion** — honest scope upfront saves quarterly audit pain.

### Outline C: "Leather export moisture protection" (for packaging / industry / textile trade press)

1. **Lead** — single moldy carton = full reject + return + reputation damage. Math.
2. **Why leather is moisture-sensitive** — tanning residues as mold substrate.
3. **Sizing math for leather export** — base sizing + multiplier on long-haul routes.
4. **Pre-pack discipline** — kiln-drying, breathable bags, kiln-dried pallets.
5. **Container-level program** — strips per container on tropical-to-temperate routes.
6. **Indicating silica gel for QC** — visual saturation verification at destination.
7. **Honest scope** — what ISO 9001 + DMF-free covers; what it doesn't.
8. **Conclusion** — packaging discipline + desiccant program = reliable arrival.

## 5. Outreach process (use this every time)

**Step 1 — Identify the right editor.**
Don't email "info@" or "editor@". Find the actual editor responsible for the section your pitch fits. LinkedIn search: "[Publication name] editor" or "[Publication name] contributor manager". Verify their current role; editors move often.

**Step 2 — Personalize the first line.**
Reference a recent piece they published. One sentence is enough. "I read your piece on [topic] last month — the angle on [specific point] is what we see consistently from the shipper side too."

**Step 3 — Send the pitch (from this kit, lightly edited).**

**Step 4 — Wait 7 days. Then send ONE follow-up.**
Subject: "Quick follow-up: [original subject]". Body: 4 lines. "Hi [name], wanted to check if my pitch landed. Happy to adjust the angle or scope if your editorial calendar has different priorities. Original pitch below for reference."

**Step 5 — Track in a spreadsheet.**
| Publication | Editor name | Pitch sent date | Follow-up date | Status | Result |
Track every pitch even if it doesn't land — the database compounds.

**Step 6 — When accepted, deliver fast.**
Editors hire writers who hit deadlines. First piece in on time even if the second piece needs polish.

## 6. What NOT to do

- **Don't mass-email** the same pitch to 50 publications. Editors share notes. One generic mass-pitch poisons the well across the publication network.
- **Don't pitch a product piece** disguised as editorial. Even if the editor accepts it, readers see through it and the article doesn't drive backlinks — it drives bounce rate.
- **Don't pay for placement** at "premium guest post" mills. Most are toxic-domain content farms. Google's link spam algorithm flags them automatically.
- **Don't lie about certifications.** Coverage that frames the company as FDA-certified or food-grade-certified is editorial inaccuracy that gets corrected — and when it is, the correction notice damages the brand permanently. Stick to ISO 9001:2015 + DMF-free + 60+ markets + 1983.
- **Don't trade reciprocal guest posts** (you publish on theirs, they publish on yours). Google's reciprocity detector catches the pattern.
